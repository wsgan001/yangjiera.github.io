About

The new waive of AI can be mostly attributed to the advancement of the theories in formulating real-world problems as abstract and formal tasks, the computational power of modern computers, and the increasing amount of task-related data. Machines, however, are still not good at many tasks humans excel at, e.g. conversation, disease diagnosis, etc. These tasks usually require intuitive, subjective, or specialist knowledge, which is possessed by most or some individuals. The complementarity between machine and human capabilities, are likely to push AI systems to the next generation - human-machine collaborative computing - to solve more complex tasks.
We maintains an up-to-date page on human-machine collaborative computing including perspectives, case studies, and literature.

Perspectives

  • There are different roles humans can play in human-machine partnership. Humans can either provide direct solutions for problem-solving, or contribute data for better training machines, e.g., through crowdsourcing.
  • Eric Horvitz propose in his recently talk "Challenges and Opportunities in Human-Machine Partnership" (July 18, 2017) that the core directions for human-machine partnership includes the following: 1) models of people and tasks, 2) models of complentarity, and 3) coordination of initiative. In particular, he proposes to represent the models of the world built by the system, and by human beliefs as probabilistic distribution.
